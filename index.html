Accumulated Cyclone Energy (ACE) measures the intensity or an individual tropical cyclone throughout its entire lifespan. It is computed by adding the sum of the squares of a storm's maximum sustained winds (in knots) at four distinct, six-hour time intervals (00 UTC, 06 UTC, 12 UTC, 18 UTC). A season's ACE is therefore calculated by summing the ACE produced by all of its storms. An average hurricane season normally produces an ACE of 110, with the 2005 Atlantic Hurricane Season generating the highest ACE value on record for the Western Hemisphere -- 250 -- for a single season.

Of note, Hurricane Ioke (2006) holds the record for the highest ACE value with 82 as it traversed the Central Pacific Basin. Closer to home, the San Ciriaco Hurricane (1899) holds the Atlantic Basin's record with an ACE value of 73.6. While Hurricane Katrina (2005) and Hurricane Patricia (2015) were the most destructive and intense hurricanes ever measured, respectively, their relatively short lifespans as cyclones limited their total ACE. Katrina registered an ACE of 20, while Patricia's ACE value was 17.885.
